# Lessons Learned

## Project reflection

This project gave me practical experience planning and carrying out an audio measurement and validation study using professional laboratory equipment. Although I had prior exposure to audio measurement, this was an opportunity to take greater responsibility for developing the test plan, configuring the systems, collecting measurements, interpreting the results, and producing a complete technical report.

## Primary challenges

The most difficult part of the project was the calibration and signal-routing process. The Studer tape machine had to be moved into the testing space and integrated correctly with the Audio Precision system, studio computer, audio interface, and existing room connections.

The experiment also had to be completed within a limited period using a studio and computer that were not part of my normal working environment. This created additional pressure around:

- Configuring unfamiliar equipment and routing
- Calibrating the tape machine correctly
- Verifying the complete signal path
- Collecting usable measurements efficiently
- Saving and organizing files
- Producing clear graphs with limited equipment access

These constraints demonstrated that measurement work depends not only on knowing how to operate an analyzer, but also on preparation, routing discipline, calibration, time management, and data organization.

## What went well

One of the strongest parts of the project was the planning process. The experimental design was developed and adjusted as the test date approached, allowing the study to be completed despite the access and scheduling limitations.

The project ultimately produced a substantial technical report and several meaningful findings. In particular, the low-frequency difference at 15 IPS and the THD+N difference at 30 IPS showed that the comparison could reveal behavior that was not obvious from general assumptions about tape emulation.

The quality of the available equipment also strengthened the study. Working with a well-maintained Studer A80 provided a rare opportunity to test professional analog equipment that is increasingly difficult to access. The project also used an appropriate MRL calibration tape and high-quality recording tape, creating a strong hardware basis for the comparison.

## What I would improve

If repeating the experiment, I would place greater emphasis on scheduling and data management.

Specific improvements would include:

- Reserving more time for setup and calibration
- Allowing additional time in the room with the equipment
- Performing a more detailed routing review before the test session
- Establishing file names and folder structures in advance
- Saving measurement exports and graph files immediately
- Maintaining redundant copies of the collected data
- Reserving time to repeat questionable measurements
- Documenting settings and changes throughout the session

These steps would reduce time pressure, improve repeatability, and make the resulting analysis easier to verify and extend.

## Engineering development

The project deepened my understanding of how professional audio measurement and product-validation work is performed. It showed me that reliable results require more than generating a graph: the equipment must be calibrated, the signal path must be understood, operating conditions must be controlled, and unexpected results must be checked before conclusions are drawn.

Most importantly, the experience increased my ability to take ownership of a measurement project—from planning and system setup through analysis and technical communication. That process is directly applicable to future work in audio-product testing, DSP validation, electroacoustics, automotive audio, and engineering quality assurance.
