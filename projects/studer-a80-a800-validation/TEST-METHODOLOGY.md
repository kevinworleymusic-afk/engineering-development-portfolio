# Test Methodology

## 1. Test objective

The experiment compared the measurable performance of a Studer A80 RC MkII 1/4-inch two-track analog tape recorder with the Universal Audio Studer A800 Multichannel Tape Recorder plug-in.

Testing evaluated three characteristics:

- Frequency response
- Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
- Total harmonic distortion plus noise (THD+N)

Each system was measured in input mode and under 15 and 30 inches-per-second (IPS) operating conditions.

## 2. Measurement equipment

- Audio Precision SYS 2522 System Two Cascade Dual Domain analyzer
- Studer A80 RC MkII analog tape recorder
- Universal Audio Studer A800 Multichannel Tape Recorder plug-in, version 1.10
- Emtec Studio Master 900 maxima 1/4-inch tape
- MRL 250 nWb/m calibration tape
- Apollo x8p audio interface
- Pro Tools
- Balanced XLR connections and studio tie lines

## 3. Hardware configuration

The Audio Precision analyzer output was connected by XLR to the Studer A80 input. The tape-machine output was returned to the measurement system so that its response could be analyzed.

A new reel of Emtec Studio Master 900 maxima tape was used throughout the hardware testing. This formulation was selected because a corresponding tape option was available in the plug-in.

## 4. Hardware calibration

The Studer A80 reproduce head was calibrated separately at 15 and 30 IPS using an MRL 250 nWb/m calibration tape.

Calibration tones included:

- 100 Hz
- 1 kHz
- 10 kHz

Audio Precision sine-wave signals were then used to align the record head to an operating level of 520 nWb/m for both tape speeds.

Calibration and signal levels were checked before the final measurements were collected.

## 5. Plug-in configuration

The Studer A800 plug-in was inserted on an otherwise empty Pro Tools track. The Audio Precision signal was routed through studio tie lines 13 and 14 to line inputs 1 and 2 of an Apollo x8p. The processed Pro Tools output was then returned to the measurement system.

The test used the following documented settings:

- Studio Master 900 tape formulation
- NAB equalization
- Noise enabled
- Automatic calibration enabled
- Apollo x8p operating at 48 kHz and 24-bit resolution

The plug-in was recalibrated when changing operating conditions. Signal flow and levels were verified before measurements were taken.

## 6. Measurement procedure

The hardware was measured first, followed by the plug-in.

For each system, testing was conducted in this order:

1. Select the operating condition: input, 15 IPS, or 30 IPS.
2. Confirm the required calibration and signal level.
3. Run an Audio Precision sine sweep through the complete signal path.
4. Capture the resulting frequency response.
5. Record SNR and THD+N under the same operating condition.
6. Change to the next operating condition and repeat the process.

After completing the Studer A80 measurements, the studio routing was changed and the same measurement sequence was performed on the A800 plug-in.

## 7. Comparison approach

Results from corresponding hardware and plug-in modes were compared for:

- Overall frequency-response shape
- Frequency ranges containing measurable differences
- SNR consistency across operating conditions
- THD+N agreement or deviation
- Behavior specific to 15 and 30 IPS operation

The study evaluated whether the systems produced functionally similar measurements rather than assuming that the plug-in would duplicate every characteristic of an individual analog machine.

## 8. Scope and limitations

The results represent one Studer A80, one reel of tape, one plug-in configuration, and the documented measurement system.

Analog tape-machine performance can vary because of:

- Calibration
- Component tolerances
- Machine age and condition
- Head wear and alignment
- Tape condition and formulation
- Maintenance history

The experiment did not include repeated trials, statistical uncertainty analysis, multiple hardware units, alternative tape formulations, or controlled listening tests. Therefore, its findings should be treated as a case study of the tested configuration rather than a universal characterization of every Studer A80 or tape-emulation plug-in.
